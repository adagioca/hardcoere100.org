Results and Recaps

1 big loop, 100 miles, +20,000 ft of elevation gain and loss

The 100 mile route is shown on the map below, at the bottom of this page. If you plan to use a GPS to navigate, please use this GPX file and a cue sheet. As of 2012, there is also a metric version - 100 km, or about 63 miles, long (GPX file). The 2013 edition will feature the same 100k course.

The start is at the Hunting Hollow parking lot (indicated by the red marker on the map) - note that there is a $6 parking fee. Here's a short hand notation of the route and trails covered:

Advice: don't think about attempting this unless you are able to navigate on your own (using maps, GPS or memory) and get out of Coe by yourself if needed. It is not a good place to take wrong turns, get lost or rely on others for your well-being.

Route highlights

The route can be thought of to consist of 4 legs: 1) Hunting Hollow (HH) to Headquarters (HQ), 2) HQ to Pacheco Camp (PC), 3) PC past Dowdy Ranch (DR) to the top of Wagon road, and 4) Wagon back to HH.

Regarding logistics: both HQ and DR have potable water (no need for filtering), HQ has a coffee and vending machine (accessible during office hours), PC has a reliable source of water but needs filtering or chemical treatment, and there are many small ponds and lakes along the way with varying degrees of water quality and level that can be used to source water for filtering.

Coe hazards (or highlights, depending how you look at it) include mountain lions, rattlesnakes, wild boar, deer, ticks, poison oak, sharp snagging branches and other overgrowth, and of course: brutally steep trails.

Click below for more detail on the various parts of the route:

[+] Leg 1: HH to HQ (36 miles)

A lot of slow, hard stuff (the Lyman-Willson climb, the Cross Canyon climb(s), the Mahoney Meadows wall to Lost Spring) is served up front. Some of the more technical descents such as Timm* and the traverse of Cross Canyon should provide a good challenge. Cross Canyon, Willow Ridge trail and China Hole offer awesome and fast descents.

(*) Sadly, in early 2012 Timm trail has been somewhat 'neutered', but it remains a fun and flowy descent.

[+] Leg 2: HQ to PC (24 miles)

This part combines one of the best and longest descents of the day (Middle Ridge) with arguably the most grueling climb (Bear Mountain). After scaling Bear Mountain, the route drops the riders to Mississippi Lake (another good source of water - requiring filtering); subsequently, at times primitive trails (Heritage, upper Pacheco Creek trail) lead back southwards to Pacheco Camp.

[+] Leg 3: PC to top Wagon (20 miles)

Probably the make-it or break-it part. It features a deep dive into the Coe backcountry, with as main highlight undoubtedly Dutch's trail: a truly great descent, with some short but stinging climbs mixed in. Near North Fork trail one ends up at the lowest point of the entire route (in elevation, possibly also in morale), leaving a brutal, no-prisoners-taken climb out to Dowdy Ranch and beyond. Burra Burra trail provides some fun singletrack, and a break from the climbing action, but Center (non-)Flats road has a tendency to squeeze all remaining energy out of ones legs.

[+] Leg 4: Wagon to HH (20 miles)

The 'easy' leg, and something to look forward to during the butchery of the preceding miles: it features mostly gradual (for Coe standards) fireroad climbs, and highly entertaining, fun singletrack descents (Kelly Lake trail, Dexter trail), with a long descent home on Wagon road. Oh yes, but with a 500ft tall twist at the end.

Why this time of the year?

A tradeoff: in spring, there may be a lot of water running through the various creeks and otherwise easy trail sections or creekbeds may become serious barriers - at the least they'll slow you down quite a bit. Also, trails tend to get overgrown, poison oak (everyones favorite weed) can be rampant and there's an army of ticks out. But there is often also a great wildflower show. Dry periods during the winter would work but the days are short. In summer it gets brutally hot. So fall seems to be a good compromise.

How long will it take me?

Coe tends to slow one down - too many bumpy and steep trails, wildlife running in the way, assorted hazards and nice photo opportunities. Fast experienced riders should count on 15-16 hours (self-supported). Check the recaps for more insight - it may take over 20 hours. You may be a lot faster though, only one way to find out!

Update 2011/09/25: an update on the conditions out there after some recon rides the last few weeks:

  • Unlike our ride last year (which took place mid-November), the trails are bone-dry. It hasn't rained here in over three months and you should expect loose, slippery conditions, and poor traction on many descents, so be careful.
  • There are several late-season downed trees on the course (e.g. on the descent into Cross Canyon), so again, be prepared and careful.
  • It may be hot out there - there is water available in the locations mentioned below, and in addition it can also be filtered from e.g. Mississippi Lake (~mile 50), Coit Lake (~mile 83) and probably Camp Willson (~mile 91). Also, it is very easy to get lost if you're not familiar with the park - if that's the case make sure to carry a good map and/or a GPS, with enough battery power to last a very long time. Again (you know the drill), be careful, heat stroke is no joke. Cellphone reception in Henry Coe park is generally intermittent (varies from having a strong signal on some ridges and peaks, to nothing in the canyons).
  • There was an army of gnats and flies out there the last weeks which can be very annoying during breaks or slow climbs; consider bringing some deterrant (e.g. Deet spray). Also, don't bring socks or leg warmers you're emotionally attached to - they will be covered in resilient little burrs. There is also a lot of poison oak near or on some trails - right now the 'evil weed' is fairly easy to spot as it often has turned bright red. It probably does not need mention, but stay away from it if you're sensitive to it.
  • Bike choice: hardtails will do ok on the terrain though there will be some bumpy sections and descents where you'll be dreaming of full suspension. A light full suspension bike is probably ideal. Singlespeeders should expect to walk many climbs (20% grades are very common), unless you have a very small gear. Tires: remember the loose conditions. Goatheads (puncturevine) and their ilk are very rare in Coe, though yesterday one of my riding partners suffered a flat from one.
  • Bowl trail (at the very beginning of the course) now has a new bypass section around the pond with the washout. You can either stay on the old trail (which is recorded in the GPX-file) and walk around the washout, or ride the new bypass (recommended). The bypass splits off to the left side of the old trail and runs above the pond.
  • By sheer coincidence, the Tarantula Festival takes place on October 1st at Coe Headquarters. You could buy food tickets ahead of time to get food from their BBQ and refuel - expect there may be a crowd (and line) though. The riders hit HQ at around mile 35. Food will be served there starting at 11.30am.
